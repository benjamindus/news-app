## Fusion Energy

### 1. Helion Energy Reaches 150 Million Degrees in Race to Deliver Fusion Power by 2028 - *Feb 13*

> Private fusion startup achieves critical temperature milestone in pulsed fusion approach, advancing toward commercial power delivery under Microsoft contract.

Helion Energy announced on February 13, 2026, that its seventh-generation Polaris prototype reactor has achieved plasma temperatures exceeding 150 million degrees Celsius—approximately three-quarters of the temperature required for commercial fusion power generation. The achievement represents a major milestone for the Washington-based company as it races toward a 2028 deadline to deliver electricity to Microsoft under what would be the world's first commercial fusion power purchase agreement.

Unlike conventional tokamaks that maintain steady-state plasma in doughnut-shaped magnetic fields, Helion's approach uses pulsed magnetic compression to generate short bursts of fusion conditions in a linear configuration. The system operates by creating two plasma rings at opposite ends of a 40-foot-long chamber, accelerating them toward each other at over one million miles per hour, and compressing them together with powerful magnetic fields. This compression heats the plasma to fusion temperatures while simultaneously generating the magnetic fields needed for confinement—a self-sustaining process that eliminates the need for expensive superconducting magnets.

Helion is already constructing Orion, its 50-megawatt commercial power plant, 130 miles from the Polaris prototype facility near Microsoft's expanding data center campus in Malaga, Washington. The company's unique approach targets deuterium-helium-3 fusion rather than the deuterium-tritium reactions pursued by most competitors. This fuel cycle produces fewer neutrons and generates electricity directly through electromagnetic induction rather than requiring steam turbines, potentially simplifying plant design and improving economics. CEO David Kirtley stated that reaching 150 million degrees validates the physics underlying Helion's approach and positions the company to meet its aggressive 2028 timeline. While skeptics question whether any fusion company can achieve grid delivery within three years, Helion's progress has attracted over $2 billion in funding from investors including Sam Altman, Peter Thiel, and major institutional investors betting on fusion's commercial viability.

### 2. Inertial Fusion Startup Inertia Raises $450 Million to Commercialize Laser-Based Approach - *Feb 11*

> Investment targets only fusion method proven to achieve net energy gain, aiming to bring National Ignition Facility breakthrough to commercial scale.

Inertia Enterprises secured $450 million in Series B financing on February 11, 2026, to commercialize inertial confinement fusion—the approach demonstrated by Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory's National Ignition Facility when it achieved the first controlled fusion reaction producing more energy than consumed in December 2022. The funding round positions Inertia among the most heavily capitalized private fusion ventures and validates investor confidence in laser-driven fusion as a path to commercial power generation.

Inertial confinement fusion works fundamentally differently from magnetic confinement tokamaks. Rather than holding plasma in magnetic bottles for extended periods, inertial fusion uses hundreds of powerful lasers focused simultaneously on a tiny fuel capsule to compress and heat it so rapidly that fusion reactions occur before the plasma can disperse. NIF achieved ignition by focusing 192 laser beams delivering 2.05 megajoules of energy onto a gold cylinder containing a frozen deuterium-tritium pellet smaller than a peppercorn. The resulting implosion generated temperatures exceeding 100 million degrees and pressures exceeding 100 billion Earth atmospheres for billionths of a second—conditions replicating the cores of stars—producing 3.15 megajoules of fusion energy.

Inertia's challenge is scaling this laboratory demonstration into a commercial power plant that can fire laser pulses rapidly and reliably while achieving even higher energy gains. The company is developing high-repetition-rate laser systems capable of firing multiple times per second rather than NIF's few shots per day, along with automated target fabrication and injection systems to deliver fuel capsules into the reaction chamber. Inertia CEO emphasized that "inertial fusion is the only approach proven to work"—a reference to NIF's multiple demonstrations of net energy gain compared to magnetic fusion's still-aspirational breakeven. The $450 million will fund construction of Inertia's first prototype power plant and development of advanced laser technologies. Competing inertial fusion companies including Marvel Fusion and Focused Energy are pursuing similar strategies, creating a race to determine whether laser fusion or magnetic confinement will reach commercial viability first.

### 3. Commonwealth Fusion Systems Completes First SPARC Magnet Installation - *Jan 31*

> High-temperature superconducting toroidal field magnet positioned in tokamak assembly marks progress toward 2026 first plasma target.

Commonwealth Fusion Systems achieved a critical engineering milestone in late January 2026 by successfully installing the first of eighteen high-temperature superconducting toroidal field magnets into SPARC's main assembly structure at its Devens, Massachusetts facility. The installation represents a major step toward completing the demonstration tokamak that CFS expects will produce first plasma in 2026 and achieve net fusion energy—generating more power than required to heat the plasma—in 2027.

SPARC's magnets represent the technological breakthrough enabling the compact tokamak's extraordinary performance targets. Each toroidal field magnet uses rare-earth barium copper oxide (REBCO) high-temperature superconducting tape capable of generating magnetic fields exceeding 20 Tesla—roughly twice the field strength achievable with conventional low-temperature superconductors and comparable to the most powerful research magnets on Earth. This dramatically stronger magnetic field allows SPARC to confine fusion-grade plasma in a device with only 1/65th the volume of ITER while targeting a fusion gain (Q) of approximately 11, meaning it will produce eleven times more fusion energy than the heating power supplied to the plasma.

The eighteen toroidal field magnets, each standing roughly two stories tall and weighing several tons, form the primary magnetic cage confining SPARC's plasma. Manufacturing these magnets required innovations in superconducting tape production, precision winding techniques, and cryogenic systems that maintain the superconductors at operating temperatures near -253°C. Installation must achieve tolerances measured in millimeters to ensure the magnetic field has the precise symmetry required for stable plasma confinement. Bob Mumgaard, CFS CEO, described the first magnet installation as a "historic moment" validating years of magnet development and manufacturing. If SPARC succeeds in demonstrating net fusion energy, it will validate the physics and engineering for ARC, CFS's planned 400-megawatt commercial fusion power plant targeted for grid delivery in the early 2030s. The company's partnership with MIT and over $2 billion in funding from investors including Bill Gates and Google position it as a leader in the global race toward commercial fusion power.

### 4. ITER Achieves Major Assembly Milestone with Vacuum Vessel Sector Installation - *Feb 12*

> International fusion megaproject advances tokamak construction as first vacuum vessel sector upended and positioned for assembly in France.

The international ITER fusion project announced successful completion of critical assembly operations on February 12, 2026, including the precision upending of the first 440-ton vacuum vessel sector and installation of gravity support structures in the tokamak pit at the construction site in southern France. The milestone demonstrates continued progress on the world's largest magnetic confinement fusion experiment despite schedule delays and budget pressures affecting the multinational collaboration.

ITER's vacuum vessel forms the heart of the tokamak, providing the ultra-high vacuum environment required for plasma operations while serving as the first confinement barrier for fusion reactions. The vessel consists of nine sectors, each manufactured separately and assembled on-site into a doughnut-shaped structure standing 19 meters tall and weighing over 5,000 tons when complete. Vacuum Vessel Sector #1 arrived at ITER horizontally and required careful rotation to vertical orientation using specialized tooling before transfer to the assembly area—a highly precise operation given the component's massive size and tight tolerances required for proper fitment.

Simultaneously, crews successfully installed the first vacuum vessel gravity support structure designed to bear the vessel's enormous weight while accommodating thermal expansion as components heat and cool during operations. The support system must maintain precise positioning despite temperature variations and mechanical loads from electromagnetic forces during plasma discharges. ITER also announced seventeen new postdoctoral fellowship opportunities for 2026 in areas including integrated plasma scenario design, artificial intelligence for fusion systems, and robotics for remote handling of activated components.

ITER's current schedule targets first plasma operations in 2035, approximately a decade later than original projections due to manufacturing challenges, regulatory requirements, and coordination complexities among the seven member entities (Europe, United States, China, Japan, South Korea, India, and Russia) contributing components and funding. The project's $22+ billion cost and extended timeline have intensified debates about whether large government-led fusion projects can compete with nimbler private ventures like Commonwealth Fusion Systems and Helion. However, ITER advocates emphasize the facility's unique scale and comprehensive physics mission will provide data and operational experience critical for all future fusion power plants regardless of which design ultimately achieves commercial success.

### 1. Gene-Edited CAR-T Cells Achieve Three-Year Remissions in Aggressive T-Cell Leukemia - *Feb 10*

> Base-edited universal CAR-T therapy produces sustained remissions in previously untreatable blood cancer, with 64% of patients remaining disease-free.

Researchers at University College London and Great Ormond Street Hospital reported on February 10, 2026, that a gene-edited CAR-T cell therapy is producing deep, long-lasting remissions in patients with T-cell acute lymphoblastic leukemia—an aggressive blood cancer that has historically resisted standard CAR-T approaches. Early trial results show 64% of treated patients remain free of leukemia three years after treatment, with the earliest participants now disease-free and off therapy for their entire follow-up period.

The therapy represents a breakthrough in addressing a fundamental challenge that has prevented CAR-T cells from treating T-cell malignancies. Standard CAR-T therapies work by engineering a patient's T cells to attack cancer cells displaying specific surface proteins. This approach has revolutionized treatment of B-cell leukemias and lymphomas, but fails against T-cell cancers because the engineered CAR-T cells would attack each other—they're made from the same cell type as the cancer. The UCL team solved this problem using base editing, a precision gene-editing technology that rewrites specific DNA letters without cutting the double helix.

The researchers used base editors to make two critical modifications to donor T cells, creating "universal" CAR-T cells capable of attacking T-cell leukemia without self-destructing. First, they disabled the T-cell receptor gene so the engineered cells won't trigger graft-versus-host disease in recipients. Second, they removed CD7, the surface protein targeted by the CAR, from the therapeutic cells themselves—enabling them to destroy cancer cells displaying CD7 while remaining immune to their own attack. Additional edits disabled genes that would trigger immune rejection, allowing a single manufactured batch to treat multiple patients rather than requiring individual customization.

Trial participants had exhausted standard treatments including chemotherapy, bone marrow transplants, and earlier-generation therapies. Many entered the study with active disease after multiple relapses. Following a single infusion of base-edited CAR-T cells, 64% achieved complete remission that has now persisted for three years—an unprecedented outcome for this patient population. The therapy's success validates base editing as safer than conventional CRISPR-Cas9 gene editing, which cuts DNA and risks chromosomal rearrangements. Base editing chemically converts specific DNA bases (for example, changing a C to a T) with precision comparable to "find and replace" in a word processor. Multiple biotechnology companies are now adapting base-edited CAR-T platforms for other cancers, potentially expanding these transformative therapies to solid tumors and autoimmune diseases where conventional CAR-T approaches have struggled.

### 2. Australian Trial Launches First Personalized mRNA Cancer Vaccines for Pediatric Brain Tumors - *Feb 10*

> $2.6 million government-funded study tests neoantigen vaccines in children with treatment-resistant gliomas across eight hospitals nationwide.

The University of Queensland launched the PaedNEO-VAX clinical trial on February 10, 2026—the world's first study testing personalized mRNA cancer vaccines specifically designed for children with advanced or treatment-resistant brain tumors. The $2.578 million trial, funded by the Australian Government and developed in partnership with Providence Therapeutics' mRNA platform, will enroll patients at eight pediatric hospitals across Queensland, South Australia, New South Wales, Victoria, and Western Australia.

The trial targets pediatric high-grade gliomas—aggressive brain tumors that carry approximately 50% five-year survival rates even with intensive treatment. These cancers resist conventional therapies because they develop in the brain's eloquent areas controlling critical functions, limiting surgical resection, and because tumor cells evolve mechanisms to evade immune detection. Personalized neoantigen vaccines offer a fundamentally different approach: training each patient's immune system to recognize and destroy their unique cancer cells based on tumor-specific mutations.

The vaccine creation process begins with comprehensive genomic sequencing of each patient's tumor and normal tissue to identify neoantigens—mutated proteins displayed only on cancer cells. Algorithms predict which neoantigens will trigger the strongest immune responses, then those sequences are encoded into mRNA molecules packaged in lipid nanoparticles. When injected, the vaccine delivers genetic instructions causing the patient's cells to temporarily produce the tumor neoantigens, training T cells to recognize and attack any cells displaying those cancer-specific markers. This approach combines the precision of genomic medicine with mRNA vaccine technology validated during the COVID-19 pandemic.

Manufacturing timelines have improved dramatically through AI-assisted neoantigen selection and automated mRNA synthesis, reducing production from nine weeks to under four weeks. This speed is critical for aggressive pediatric brain tumors where delays can allow disease progression. The trial will test vaccine safety, immune response generation, and preliminary efficacy signals in combination with standard therapies. Similar personalized mRNA vaccine approaches in adult melanoma have demonstrated 49% reduction in recurrence risk when combined with checkpoint inhibitors, with benefits persisting for five years. If successful in pediatric brain tumors, the approach could extend to other childhood cancers including neuroblastoma, sarcomas, and treatment-resistant leukemias. Regulatory approvals for personalized cancer vaccines could begin in late 2026 to 2027 contingent on pivotal Phase III results, potentially transforming cancer treatment from one-size-fits-all protocols to individually tailored immunotherapies.

### 3. FDA Poised to Approve First Gene Therapy for Hunter Syndrome Despite Clinical Hold Controversy - *Feb 8*

> Clemidsogene lanparvovec decision deadline arrives amid unresolved questions about cancer risk in related gene therapy program.

The FDA faces a critical regulatory decision on February 8, 2026, regarding approval of clemidsogene lanparvovec (RGX-121)—the first gene therapy directly addressing the genetic cause of mucopolysaccharidosis II (Hunter syndrome). The application arrives under controversial circumstances, as the FDA placed a clinical hold on Regenxbio's related MPS therapy program following a cancer case under investigation to determine potential links to gene therapy.

Hunter syndrome is a rare X-linked recessive disorder caused by deficiency of the lysosomal enzyme iduronate-2-sulfatase (IDS), leading to accumulation of glycosaminoglycans throughout the body. Patients experience progressive organ dysfunction, skeletal abnormalities, respiratory complications, and in severe cases, profound neurological deterioration beginning in early childhood. Current treatment consists of lifelong weekly enzyme replacement infusions that cannot cross the blood-brain barrier, leaving neurological disease untreated. RGX-121 offers a fundamentally different approach: a one-time intrathecal (spinal) infusion of adeno-associated virus carrying a functional IDS gene directly into the central nervous system.

The approval application is supported by biomarker and clinical data from the CAMPSIITE trial, which enrolled children aged 4 months to 5 years with severe Hunter syndrome. Results showed an 82% reduction in cerebrospinal fluid heparan sulfate—the toxic molecule that accumulates in the brain and drives neurological symptoms—at 12 months post-treatment. Additionally, treated children demonstrated stabilization or improvement in neurodevelopmental assessments compared to the expected decline in untreated patients, suggesting the therapy may preserve cognitive function if administered before irreversible brain damage occurs.

However, the approval decision is complicated by the clinical hold on RGX-111, Regenxbio's therapy for the related disorder MPS I (Hurler syndrome). An investigation is underway after one patient developed cancer, though no evidence of malignancy has emerged in the nine other RGX-111 trial participants or any of the RGX-121 Hunter syndrome patients. Gene therapy safety concerns focus on potential insertional mutagenesis—where the therapeutic virus integrates into chromosomes and disrupts cancer suppressor genes—and immune responses to viral vectors. AAV vectors are generally considered safer than older retroviral approaches because they rarely integrate into host DNA, but the field remains vigilant following historical gene therapy tragedies.

The Hunter syndrome patient community urgently advocates for approval despite uncertainty, emphasizing that affected children face progressive, devastating disease with no curative alternatives. If approved, RGX-121 would join a growing roster of gene therapies for rare diseases including spinal muscular atrophy, inherited retinal dystrophies, and hemophilia—conditions where single gene corrections can dramatically alter disease trajectories. The decision will signal FDA's risk tolerance for gene therapies in severe pediatric diseases where conventional treatments offer limited benefit.

### 4. Mount Sinai Researchers Engineer CAR-T Cells Targeting Tumor "Bodyguard" Macrophages - *Jan 28*

> Novel immunotherapy eliminates cancer-protecting immune cells in preclinical models, opening tumors to immune attack and eradicating lung and ovarian cancers in mice.

Scientists at the Icahn School of Medicine at Mount Sinai published research on January 28, 2026, demonstrating a fundamentally new cancer immunotherapy approach: engineering CAR-T cells to eliminate tumor-associated macrophages—immune cells that cancers hijack as protective shields against the body's anti-tumor defenses. The strategy produced complete tumor eradication in mouse models of aggressive metastatic lung and ovarian cancers, suggesting a potential breakthrough for solid tumors that have largely resisted conventional CAR-T therapies.

Tumors don't grow in isolation—they recruit surrounding cells to create a protective microenvironment that enables growth and metastasis while suppressing immune attack. Macrophages, normally serving as the immune system's cleanup crew that engulfs pathogens and cellular debris, become corrupted inside tumors into "M2" or "tumor-associated" macrophages that actively suppress T cell function, promote blood vessel formation feeding tumor growth, and physically shield cancer cells from immune surveillance. As long as these bodyguards remain in place, even powerful immunotherapies struggle to access and destroy cancer cells.

The Mount Sinai team engineered CAR-T cells recognizing TREM2, a surface protein highly expressed on tumor-associated macrophages but largely absent from healthy tissues. When infused into tumor-bearing mice, these TREM2-targeted CAR-T cells hunted down and eliminated the protective macrophage populations. With their shields removed, tumors became vulnerable to the host's natural anti-tumor immunity, which flooded in and destroyed the cancer cells. Critically, eliminating macrophages didn't simply slow tumor growth—it triggered complete tumor regression in models of lung adenocarcinoma and ovarian cancer, diseases notoriously resistant to immunotherapy.

The approach addresses a major limitation of conventional CAR-T therapy: solid tumors have proven far more difficult to treat than blood cancers because of their protective microenvironments. Previous CAR-T attempts against solid tumors targeted cancer cells directly but achieved limited success because the immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment prevented CAR-T cells from functioning effectively. By first clearing the protective macrophages, the new strategy makes tumors vulnerable to both CAR-T attack and the patient's own revitalized immune system. The Mount Sinai team is optimizing the therapy for clinical translation, including engineering CAR-T cells that can survive and function in the hostile tumor microenvironment and developing manufacturing processes for clinical-grade production. If successful in human trials, macrophage-targeting immunotherapy could expand CAR-T's transformative impact beyond blood cancers to the solid tumors—lung, ovarian, pancreatic, colorectal—that account for over 90% of cancer deaths.

### 5. East Asian Study Identifies 29,000 Novel DNA Methylation Sites Enriched for Disease Risk - *Feb 10*

> Methylation quantitative trait locus analysis uncovers ancestry-specific epigenetic regulation patterns critical for interpreting disease genetics.

An international research team published findings on February 10, 2026, reporting the identification of nearly 29,000 previously unknown methylated DNA sites in East Asian populations and demonstrating that methylation quantitative trait loci (mQTLs)—genetic variants influencing where DNA methylation occurs—are strongly enriched for disease heritability, particularly when genetic ancestry matches between study populations and disease genome-wide association studies. The research, published in Nature Communications, provides critical insights into how genetic variation regulates gene expression through epigenetic mechanisms.

DNA methylation—the addition of methyl chemical groups to cytosine bases in DNA—serves as a master regulatory switch controlling when and where genes are expressed. Methylation patterns vary dramatically between cell types (explaining why liver cells differ from neurons despite identical DNA sequences) and change in response to environmental exposures, aging, and disease. mQTLs are inherited genetic variants that influence whether specific DNA locations become methylated, effectively linking genetic variation to gene regulation. Understanding mQTLs is critical for interpreting genome-wide association studies, which identify genetic variants associated with diseases but often struggle to explain how those variants cause pathology.

The study analyzed DNA methylation patterns and genetic variants in large East Asian cohorts, revealing that most previously described mQTLs were discovered in European-ancestry populations and fail to capture regulatory variation specific to other ancestries. The 29,000 newly identified methylation sites show East Asian-specific regulatory patterns, and mQTLs controlling these sites are significantly enriched in genomic regions harboring disease risk variants. Critically, disease heritability enrichment was strongest when mQTL ancestry matched the ancestry of genome-wide association study participants—demonstrating that understanding disease genetics requires ancestry-matched regulatory maps.

The findings have immediate implications for precision medicine and drug development. Many disease-associated genetic variants identified through genome-wide association studies don't fall within protein-coding genes, initially puzzling researchers about their disease mechanisms. The answer increasingly appears to be regulatory: these variants alter mQTLs that control DNA methylation patterns, which in turn change gene expression levels. By mapping mQTLs across diverse populations, researchers can predict how specific genetic variants alter methylation and expression of nearby genes, identifying causal genes and potential drug targets. The work underscores the critical importance of including diverse ancestries in genomic research—regulatory maps derived from European populations miss ancestry-specific variants that may drive disease in underrepresented populations. Multiple research consortia are now expanding mQTL mapping to African, Latino, Indigenous, and South Asian populations to create comprehensive regulatory maps enabling truly inclusive precision medicine applicable across all human genetic diversity.
