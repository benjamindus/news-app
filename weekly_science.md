### 2. Helion Energy Achieves 150 Million Degree Milestone Racing Toward 2028 Microsoft Deadline - *Feb 13*

> Fusion startup hits critical temperature threshold using pulsed magnetic compression approach, advancing commercial power delivery timeline.

Helion Energy announced on February 13, 2026, that its seventh-generation Polaris prototype reactor achieved plasma temperatures exceeding 150 million degrees Celsius—approximately three-quarters of the temperature required for sustained commercial fusion power generation. The milestone demonstrates substantial progress toward Helion's unprecedented commitment to deliver electricity to Microsoft by 2028 under what would be the world's first commercial fusion power purchase agreement.

Helion's Polaris device achieved this blistering temperature using a fundamentally different approach than conventional tokamaks. Rather than maintaining steady-state plasma in doughnut-shaped magnetic cages requiring expensive superconducting magnets, Helion uses pulsed magnetic compression in a linear configuration. The system creates two plasma rings at opposite ends of a 40-foot chamber, accelerates them toward each other at over one million miles per hour, then compresses them together with powerful magnetic fields. This compression simultaneously heats plasma to fusion temperatures while generating the magnetic fields needed for confinement—a self-sustaining process that eliminates the need for costly external magnets. Polaris also became the first privately developed fusion machine to demonstrate measurable deuterium-tritium fusion reactions.

The temperature achievement is particularly significant because approximately 100 million degrees Celsius is considered the minimum threshold for sustained commercial fusion power, making Helion's 150 million degree milestone a critical validation of its physics approach. The company is already constructing Orion, its 50-megawatt commercial power plant, 130 miles from the Polaris facility near Microsoft's expanding data center campus in Malaga, Washington. Helion's unique approach targets deuterium-helium-3 fusion rather than the deuterium-tritium reactions most competitors pursue, producing fewer neutrons and generating electricity directly through electromagnetic induction rather than requiring steam turbines. While skeptics question whether any fusion company can achieve grid delivery within three years, Helion's progress has attracted over $2 billion in funding from investors including Sam Altman and Peter Thiel, positioning it as a frontrunner in the race to commercialize fusion energy.

This approach builds directly on the breakthrough achieved by Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory's National Ignition Facility, which in December 2022 became the first controlled fusion experiment to achieve net target energy gain by focusing 192 laser beams onto a frozen deuterium-tritium pellet smaller than a peppercorn, generating 3.15 megajoules of fusion energy from 2.05 megajoules of laser energy.

Inertia's challenge is scaling this laboratory demonstration into a commercial power plant capable of firing laser pulses rapidly and reliably while achieving even higher energy gains. Inertia intends to start construction on its first grid-scale power plant in 2030, competing with magnetic fusion approaches in the race to deliver commercial fusion energy.

### 5. Princeton Researchers Solve Plasma Rotation Mystery Critical for Reactor Longevity - *Feb 17*

> New simulations show toroidal rotation determines exhaust particle distribution, enabling engineers to design divertors that can handle decades of heat loads.

Researchers at Princeton Plasma Physics Laboratory published findings in Physical Review Letters on February 17, 2026, demonstrating that toroidal rotation—the motion of plasma particles as they move around the tokamak—plays a key role in determining exactly where plasma fuel lands in the machine's exhaust system. The discovery solves a long-standing mystery about asymmetric heat loads on divertor components and provides critical data for designing future fusion reactors capable of surviving decades of continuous operation.

The research team, led by associate research physicist Eric Emdee, used the modeling code SOLPS-ITER to simulate particle paths under different conditions in the DIII-D tokamak in California. The team tested four scenarios: with and without cross-field drifts and with and without plasma rotation. Simulations couldn't match experimental observations until researchers added one crucial ingredient: the measured core rotation of 88.4 kilometers per second. When plasma core rotation was combined with cross-field drifts, simulations finally matched experimental measurements, revealing that the rotating plasma core significantly influences edge flows and exhaust particle distribution.

Understanding what drives asymmetric exhaust distribution matters critically for designing future fusion systems because engineers need to know precisely where exhaust particles will land to build divertors capable of handling the extreme heat without degrading. Divertors must survive neutron bombardment, plasma interactions, and temperatures exceeding those on the Sun's surface while maintaining structural integrity for the reactor's operational lifetime—potentially decades of continuous service. The Princeton findings suggest that accurately predicting exhaust behavior in future fusion systems will require accounting for how the rotating plasma core influences edge flows, a connection previously underappreciated in reactor design calculations. This improved understanding allows engineers to design divertors with appropriate materials, cooling systems, and geometries positioned where heat loads will actually occur, rather than relying on simplified models that failed to capture rotation effects. The research collaboration included scientists from MIT, North Carolina State University, and multiple plasma physics laboratories, demonstrating the international scientific cooperation driving fusion energy toward commercialization.

### 4. AI-Designed CRISPR Editor OpenCRISPR-1 Achieves High Precision with Reduced Off-Target Effects - *Early 2026*

> First gene-editing enzyme entirely created with large language models shows improved efficiency and safety compared to natural CRISPR systems.

The genome-editing field entered a new era in early 2026 with the public release of OpenCRISPR-1, the first gene-editing enzyme entirely created using large language models trained on extensive natural CRISPR-Cas data. The AI-designed editor represents a novel, synthetic system distinct from any known natural CRISPR mechanism, demonstrating that machine learning can now design biological tools from first principles rather than merely optimizing natural systems.

OpenCRISPR-1 was developed by training large language models on massive datasets encompassing natural CRISPR-Cas systems, protein structures, and gene editing outcomes. The AI identified patterns in successful editing enzymes and designed a synthetic nuclease optimized for human therapeutic applications. Early reports indicate OpenCRISPR-1 achieves high editing efficiency in human cells while substantially reducing off-target activity and immunogenicity compared to natural CRISPR systems—addressing two critical barriers to safe clinical gene editing.

Off-target editing, where CRISPR enzymes cut DNA at unintended locations similar to the target sequence, poses safety risks including chromosomal rearrangements and disruption of tumor suppressor genes. Immunogenicity, the tendency of bacterial CRISPR proteins to trigger immune responses when introduced into human cells, can reduce therapeutic efficacy and cause adverse reactions. By designing a synthetic editor free from evolutionary constraints that shaped natural CRISPR systems, OpenCRISPR-1 potentially overcomes limitations inherent in repurposing bacterial immune proteins for human therapeutics. The development demonstrates convergence between AI and biotechnology, where machine learning accelerates discovery and optimization of biological tools. Multiple biotechnology companies are evaluating OpenCRISPR-1 for therapeutic applications in genetic diseases, cancer, and infectious diseases, potentially expanding the range of conditions amenable to safe, precise gene editing.

The AI-driven approach achieved material discovery approximately 200 times faster than traditional laboratory experimentation, which requires synthesizing each candidate material, characterizing its properties, and testing performance under various conditions.

The research identified 25 entirely new materials maintaining magnetic properties at high temperatures—critical for electric vehicle motors, wind turbine generators, and industrial applications where magnets must function reliably at elevated operating temperatures. Today's most powerful permanent magnets depend on rare earth elements including neodymium, dysprosium, and samarium, which are costly, largely imported from China, and increasingly difficult to secure amid geopolitical tensions. The new materials use earth-abundant elements potentially available from diverse global sources, reducing supply chain vulnerabilities while lowering costs. The breakthrough demonstrates AI's transformative potential in materials discovery, where the space of possible chemical combinations is so vast that systematic experimental exploration is impractical. By learning patterns from existing scientific literature, AI can predict promising candidates for synthesis and testing, dramatically accelerating the innovation cycle. Multiple companies are now evaluating the AI-discovered materials for commercial applications, with prototypes expected within 2-3 years if scaled manufacturing proves feasible, potentially transforming clean energy technology supply chains away from rare earth dependence toward abundant alternative materials.
