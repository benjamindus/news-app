The appreciation reflects China's strategic commitment to currency strength as a pillar of national power, with President Xi Jinping calling for the renminbi to become a "powerful currency" with global reserve currency status and the People's Bank of China indicating a commitment to "slow and orderly" appreciation as it balances export-led growth with currency internationalization goals.

The accumulation was supported by a persistently weaker U.S. dollar and gold price appreciation, with the People's Bank of China continuing to add to its gold holdings for the fifteenth consecutive month in January 2026, reaching 74.19 million fine troy ounces valued at $369.58 billion, up from $319.45 billion a month earlier.

Morgan Stanley projects the yuan will strengthen to 6.85 per U.S. dollar in the first quarter of 2026, driven by improving economic fundamentals, capital inflow dynamics, and policy support for currency appreciation. However, Western analysts caution that Xi's yuan internationalization ambitions face substantial structural barriers, including capital controls that limit convertibility, lack of transparent rule of law, and dependence on state-directed credit allocation that reduces confidence compared to dollar and euro alternatives. The yuan's share of global foreign exchange reserves stood at just 1.93% in Q3 2025 according to IMF data, a distant fourth behind the dollar (approximately 57%), euro (roughly 20%), and yen, suggesting the path to reserve currency status remains long despite recent momentum.

5. Saudi Arabia Expands Yuan Oil Settlement as Petroyuan Gains Traction Despite Petrodollar Persistence - Feb 11

China and Saudi Arabia signed a $7 billion currency swap agreement enabling direct yuan-riyal settlement, as several countries including Russia, Iran, Venezuela, UAE, and Egypt now use the petroyuan, though analysts forecast decades-long transition from dollar dominance.

China and Saudi Arabia recently signed a $7 billion currency swap agreement that allows direct settlement between the yuan (CNY) and the Saudi riyal (SAR), enabling bilateral trade to be conducted without using the U.S. dollar as an intermediary currency. The agreement marks a significant step in the gradual diversification away from exclusive dollar reliance for oil transactions, though Saudi oil exports are still settled in USD by default, preserving substantial petrodollar demand even as alternatives emerge.

As of January 2025, several countries are using the petroyuan for oil transactions, including Russia, Iran, Venezuela, Saudi Arabia, China, UAE, and Egypt, representing a meaningful expansion of yuan-based energy settlement beyond countries under U.S. sanctions. However, analysts have noted that yuan-based oil trade faces significant hurdles, with few outlets to spend accumulated RMB reserves and limited depth in yuan-denominated financial markets compared to dollar alternatives. S&P Global forecasts the petroyuan will take decades to scale meaningfully, suggesting the transition away from dollar-dominated energy markets will be gradual rather than abrupt.

Rather than a sudden overthrow of the petrodollar system, the emerging pattern reflects gradual diversification toward a multipolar currency landscape where the dollar maintains dominant but diminished share alongside growing roles for the yuan, euro, and potentially digital currency alternatives. China has successfully pushed for greater yuan-based oil trading with key partners, particularly those seeking to reduce exposure to dollar-based sanctions risks, but a complete replacement of the U.S. dollar remains unlikely in the foreseeable future. The most likely outcome is a multipolar currency system where energy transactions are settled in a basket of currencies chosen based on trade relationships, geopolitical considerations, and currency stability rather than exclusive reliance on any single dominant currency.
