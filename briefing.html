3. Federal Reserve Ends Quantitative Tightening as Balance Sheet Stabilizes at $6.6 Trillion - Dec 1, 2025

The Federal Reserve officially ended quantitative tightening on December 1, 2025, after reducing its securities holdings by $2.2 trillion since June 2022, with the balance sheet stabilizing at $6.6 trillion or 21% of GDP.

The Federal Reserve concluded its quantitative tightening program on December 1, 2025, marking the end of a three-and-a-half-year effort to normalize its balance sheet following the massive pandemic-era expansion. Since balance sheet reduction began in June 2022, the Federal Reserve's total securities holdings declined by more than $2.2 trillion, with redemptions of approximately $1.6 trillion in Treasury securities and $600 billion in agency mortgage-backed securities. As of December 31, 2025, the Fed's assets stood at $6.6 trillion, representing about 21% of GDP, down from the March 2022 peak of nearly $9 trillion but still dramatically above pre-pandemic levels of $800 billion or 6% of GDP in December 2005.

The FOMC directed the Desk to roll over at auction all principal payments from the Federal Reserve's holdings of Treasury securities and reinvest all principal payments from the Federal Reserve's holdings of agency securities into Treasury bills, signaling a shift from active balance sheet reduction to maintenance mode. The Fed will likely use reserve management purchases (RMPs) in the coming months to help maintain liquidity without affecting longer-term borrowing costs, which is a goal reserved for quantitative easing (QE). This approach allows the Fed to provide short-term liquidity support while avoiding the market distortions and inflation risks associated with large-scale asset purchases.

The end of quantitative tightening comes amid persistent inflation above the Fed's 2% target and ongoing debate about the appropriate size of the central bank's balance sheet. Some economists argue that the Fed should continue reducing its holdings to pre-pandemic levels to avoid market dependency on central bank support, while others contend that the larger balance sheet is necessary to ensure adequate financial system liquidity given the growth of the economy and financial markets since 2008. The FOMC's decision to halt QT while maintaining elevated holdings suggests a pragmatic middle ground, preserving optionality to resume asset purchases if economic conditions deteriorate while avoiding the risks of overly aggressive balance sheet reduction.
